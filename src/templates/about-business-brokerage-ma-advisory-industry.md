# Business Brokerage & Mergers and Acquisitions Advisory Industry

## Overview

The Business Brokerage and M&A Advisory industry helps business owners buy and sell privately held companies.

Advisors in this industry act as intermediaries between sellers and buyers. Their role is to guide business owners through the process of selling a company while helping buyers identify and acquire suitable businesses.

The industry exists because selling a business is complex and typically involves financial, legal, operational, and strategic considerations.

Most business owners only sell a business once in their lifetime, so they rely on professional advisors to help them navigate the process.

---

# Market Segments

The industry is typically divided into several segments based on deal size.

### Main Street

Main Street brokers handle smaller businesses.

Typical deal characteristics:

- Value: $50,000 - $2M
- Often owner-operated
- Local buyers
- Deals frequently financed with SBA loans
- Less complex deal structures

Examples of businesses:

- restaurants
- small retail stores
- small service businesses
- owner-operated trades

These deals are typically handled by **business brokers**.

---

### Lower Middle Market

The lower middle market represents larger and more complex transactions.

Typical deal characteristics:

- Value: $2M - $50M+
- Professional management teams
- Larger buyer pools
- Private equity interest
- More formal deal processes

Examples include:

- manufacturing companies
- distribution businesses
- construction firms
- business service companies
- regional service providers

These deals are often handled by **M&A advisors or investment banks**.

---

### Middle Market and Investment Banking

Deals above approximately $50M are typically handled by investment banks.

These deals often involve:

- private equity sponsors
- corporate strategic buyers
- institutional investors
- more sophisticated financing structures

---

# Role of Business Brokers

Business brokers focus primarily on selling privately held small to mid-sized businesses.

Their responsibilities include:

- valuing businesses
- preparing marketing materials
- confidentially marketing the business
- screening buyers
- negotiating deal terms
- managing the transaction process

Brokers often work with owner-operated businesses where the owner's involvement is critical to operations.

---

# Role of M&A Advisors

M&A advisors operate similarly to brokers but typically work on larger transactions.

Their responsibilities include:

- financial analysis
- business valuation
- preparing confidential information memorandums (CIMs)
- identifying strategic buyers
- managing competitive sale processes
- negotiating deal structures
- coordinating due diligence
- assisting with closing transactions

M&A advisors typically work with companies that have stronger financial reporting and larger buyer pools.

---

# Sell-Side vs Buy-Side Representation

Advisors may represent either sellers or buyers.

### Sell-Side Representation

Most business brokers work primarily on the sell-side.

In this role they represent the business owner and help them sell their company.

The advisor's responsibility is to maximize the value of the business and identify the best buyer.

---

### Buy-Side Representation

Some advisors represent buyers looking to acquire businesses.

In this role they help buyers:

- identify acquisition targets
- analyze potential deals
- negotiate acquisitions

Buy-side engagements are more common in larger M&A transactions.

---

# The Typical Deal Process

Selling a business generally follows a structured process.

### 1. Initial Consultation

The advisor learns about the business, the owner's goals, and whether the company is ready for sale.

---

### 2. Valuation

The advisor estimates the market value of the company based on financial performance and industry multiples.

Common valuation metrics include:

- Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE)
- EBITDA
- revenue multiples

---

### 3. Preparation

The advisor prepares marketing materials including:

- confidential information memorandums
- financial summaries
- operational overviews

The business is positioned for buyers.

---

### 4. Marketing

The advisor markets the opportunity confidentially to qualified buyers.

Buyers typically must sign a **Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)** before receiving detailed information.

---

### 5. Buyer Screening

Not all interested parties are qualified buyers.

Advisors screen buyers based on:

- financial capability
- acquisition experience
- strategic fit

---

### 6. Offers and Letters of Intent (LOI)

Interested buyers submit offers outlining:

- purchase price
- deal structure
- financing terms
- closing conditions

The seller selects the best offer.

---

### 7. Due Diligence

After signing an LOI, the buyer reviews the company's financial and operational records to confirm the accuracy of the information presented.

---

### 8. Closing

Attorneys prepare final purchase agreements and transfer ownership of the company to the buyer.

---

# How Advisors Get Paid

Business brokers and M&A advisors are typically compensated through success fees.

The most common structure is a **commission based on the sale price**.

Typical structures include:

### Business Brokerage

Often structured as a percentage of the sale price.

Typical range:

6% - 12%

---

### M&A Advisory

Often structured using formulas such as the **Lehman Formula**.

Example:

- 5% of the first $1M
- 4% of the second $1M
- 3% of the third $1M
- 2% of the fourth $1M
- 1% of the remainder

These formulas vary by firm.

---

# Buyer Types

Common buyers in the market include:

### Individual Buyers

Entrepreneurs looking to acquire and operate a business.

Often use SBA loans to finance acquisitions.

---

### Strategic Buyers

Companies that buy competitors or related businesses to grow market share.

These buyers may pay higher valuations due to strategic benefits.

---

### Private Equity Firms

Private equity firms buy companies to grow them and eventually sell them for a profit.

They typically focus on:

- scalable businesses
- strong management teams
- recurring revenue
- growth potential

---

### Search Funds

Entrepreneurs who raise capital from investors to acquire and operate a single business.

---

# Challenges in the Industry

Selling a business is complex and many transactions fail to close.

Common challenges include:

- unrealistic seller expectations
- poor financial records
- buyer financing issues
- due diligence problems
- deal fatigue during long transactions

Professional advisors help manage these risks.

---

# Why Advisors Add Value

Professional advisors add value by:

- creating competitive buyer interest
- managing negotiations
- protecting confidentiality
- coordinating the deal team
- increasing the likelihood of closing

Many studies show that professionally marketed businesses often sell for higher prices than those sold directly.
